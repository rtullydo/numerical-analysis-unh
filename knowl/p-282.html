One marker of nice functions in two variables is the conclusion of Clairaut's theorem, which states that the mixed partial derivatives of \(f\) are equal - that is, for a nice \(f\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x}. \end{equation*}

Suppose for the moment that a vector field

\begin{equation*} F = \langle M, N \rangle = \nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle \end{equation*}

and that the derivatives \(f_{xx}, f_{yy}, f_{xy}, f_{yx}\) all exist and are continuous. Clairaut's theorem will force

\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = f_{xy} = f_{yx} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}\text{.} \end{equation*}

It turns out to be the case that this condition, \(M_y = N_x\text{,}\) is not only necessary but sufficient on nice enough domains like rectangles.
