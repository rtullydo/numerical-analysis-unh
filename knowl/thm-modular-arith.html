Theorem 2.3.3.
\(m \geq 1\)

  1. If \(a_1 \equiv a_2 \bmod n\) and \(b_1 \equiv b_2 \bmod n\) then

    \begin{equation*} a_1 \pm b_1 \equiv a_2 \pm b_2 \bmod n \text{ and } a_1 \cdot b_1 \equiv a_2 \cdot b_2 \bmod n. \end{equation*}

  2. Let \(a\) be an integer. Then

    \begin{equation*} a \cdot b \equiv 1 \bmod n \text{ for some integer if and only if } \gcd(a,n) = 1. \end{equation*}

    If \(b\) exists, it is unique, and it is called the inverse of \(a\text{.}\)
