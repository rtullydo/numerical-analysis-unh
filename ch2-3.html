Section 2.3 Separable homogeneous equations and the substitution method

¶

Consider an ODE

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=f\left(x,y\right) \end{equation*}

and suppose we can rewrite it in the form

\begin{equation} \frac{dy}{dx}=F\left(\frac{y}{x}\right).\label{homoeq}\tag{2.3.1} \end{equation}

An equation of the form (2.3.1) is called homogeneous.

  • To solve this equation. We will define a new variable

    \begin{equation*} v=\frac{y}{x}\,\,\,\,\,\,\,(\text{Important}) \end{equation*}

    and write everything in terms of only \(v\) and \(x\text{!}\)

  • Solve for \(y\text{:}\) and get

    \begin{equation*} y=xv. \end{equation*}

  • Implicitly differentiate both sides:

    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=x\frac{dv}{dx}+1\cdot v.\,\,\,\,\,\,\,(\text{Important}) \end{equation*}

The two important equations we come up with are:

\begin{equation*} \text{General homogeneous substitution:}\begin{cases} v=\frac{y}{x}\\ \frac{dy}{dx}=x\frac{dv}{dx}+v \end{cases} \end{equation*}
Example 2.3.1.

Consider

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{x^{2}+xy+y^{2}}{x^{2}}. \end{equation*}

Part (a): Show that this ODE is homogeneous and rewrite the entire equation by only \(v\) and \(x\text{.}\)

To see this we divide the numerator and denominator by \(x^{2}\) and get

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1+\frac{y}{x}+\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)^{2}}{1}. \end{equation*}

Then replacing \(\frac{dy}{dx}=x\frac{dv}{dx}+v\) and \(v=\frac{y}{x}\text{,}\) we get a new equation

\begin{equation*} x\frac{dv}{dx}+v=1+v+v^{2}. \end{equation*}

Part (b): Solve the ODE in terms of \(v\) and then return everything into terms of \(y,x\text{.}\)

We rewrite

\begin{align*} x\frac{dv}{dx}=1+v^{2} \amp \iff\int\frac{dv}{1+v^{2}}=\int\frac{1}{x}dx\\ \amp \iff\tan^{-1}\left(v\right)=\ln\left|x\right|+c\\ \amp \iff\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)=\ln\left|x\right|+c. \end{align*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{y}{x}=\tan\left(\ln\left|x\right|+C\right) \end{equation*}

and the general solution is

\begin{equation*} y_g(x)=x\tan\left(\ln\left|x\right|+C\right) \end{equation*}
Example 2.3.2.

Find the solution to

\begin{equation*} y^{\prime}=\frac{y}{x}+\frac{x}{y}\,\,\,\,,x>0. \end{equation*}

Step 1: First check if you can apply any of the method of the previous sections (linear? separable?). The equation is neither linear, or separable. But notice that this is homogeneous for if we let \(v=\frac{y}{x}\) then

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{y}{x}+\frac{1}{y/x}=v+\frac{1}{v}. \end{equation*}

Step 2: Recall that \(\frac{dy}{dx}=x\frac{dv}{dx}+v\) , so plug this into the LHS, and get

\begin{align*} x\frac{dv}{dx}+v=v+\frac{1}{v} \amp \iff\int vdv=\int\frac{1}{x}dx\\ \amp \iff\frac{v^{2}}{2}=\ln\left|x\right|+C\\ \amp \iff y^{2}=2x^{2}\ln\left|x\right|+kx^{2}. \end{align*}

and we get the general solution

\begin{equation*} y_g(x)=\pm\sqrt{2x^{2}\ln\left|x\right|+Cx^{2}} \end{equation*}
Example 2.3.3. (not always the same substitution).

Rewrite the equation

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=e^{9y-x} \end{equation*}

in terms of only \(v,x\) by letting \(v=9y-x\text{.}\)

Solution: Use the substitution \(v=9y-x\text{,}\) then solve for \(y\) and get

\begin{equation*} y=\frac{1}{9}v+\frac{1}{9}x. \end{equation*}

Then using implicit differentiation,

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{1}{9}\frac{dv}{dx}+\frac{1}{9} \end{equation*}

and hence

\begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx}=e^{9y-x} \amp \iff\frac{1}{9}\frac{dv}{dx}+\frac{1}{9}=e^{v}\\ \amp \iff\frac{dv}{dx}=9e^{v}-1. \end{align*}

and this can be easily solved by separating variables.
